This world is no different from the one you and I know. The morning rush-hour subway is packed with bleary-eyed commuters, the food trucks outside schools always have long lines for burritos, and hospital corridors are filled with people staring blankly at lab reports. We call this era the 'Age of Mortals.' History books say that about three hundred years ago, the aether in the world suddenly dried up, and gods and spirits receded like a tide from the human realm, leaving only yellowed myths in old texts and symbolic incense during festivals. Occasionally, the elderly would tell tales of strange things their ancestors had seen, but the young just took them as stories. Yet embers never truly die. Sometimes, when you drag yourself home from work late at night, you catch a glimpse of your shadow under the streetlamp with an extra pair of wings, but when you rub your eyes, it's back to normal. In the corner of your rented old house, a faint greenish glow seeps through the wall, like fireflies gathering and scattering. The property management came to check—wiring is fine. Such incidents are too trivial and too faint to be taken seriously; post them online and people will just say you overdid the filter. Gradually, no one believes anymore. Society still runs on diplomas, connections, and bank balances; the ember of aether can't change your rent or cure a terminal illness. The few who claim to sense the embers are mostly dismissed as frauds or patients with psychological issues. The only ones who really care are some secret research institutes and underground collectors, who pay to acquire those 'aether-tinged' trinkets—an old copper coin that's always warm, a jade pendant that weeps before rain. Legend says you can trade embers for a bit of luck, but the price is often your lifespan or something far more precious. And so we live on, in ninety-nine percent of our mundane existence, occasionally touching that one percent of the inexplicable. Maybe the owner of the sandwich shop downstairs always gives you too much change—not because he's forgetful, but because the faded red string on his wrist is slightly warm. Maybe your three-year-old daughter calls out to an empty corner as if seeing a playmate—not just childish talk. But no one digs deeper, because when dawn breaks and the alarm goes off, you still have to squeeze onto the subway, pay off your mortgage, and keep living this life that seems ordinary but ripples beneath the surface.
